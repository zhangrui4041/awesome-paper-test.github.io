Introduction

Sound has long played a crucial role in human communication, yet its full potential in the realm of computing remains underexplored. With the proliferation of smart devices and the evolution of interfaces that integrate visual, tactile, and auditory elements, the field of auditory computing has emerged as a vibrant area of research that seeks to harness the power of sound in computing applications. Over the past decade, there has been a growing interest in leveraging sound not only as an output channel but also as an effective input modality. This shift towards using sound as a bidirectional interface facilitates more intuitive and natural interactions between humans and machines. This paper is motivated by the emerging possibilities sound offers to enhance human-machine interactions. Technologies such as speech recognition, sound synthesis, and auditory displays have opened new avenues for research and innovation. Speech recognition systems like those used in digital assistants (e.g., Siri, Alexa) demonstrate the potential of auditory interfaces to interpret complex vocal inputs and perform various tasks seamlessly. Similarly, sound synthesis and manipulation techniques are crucial in creating immersive environments in virtual and augmented reality settings. The scope of this paper brings into focus not only the technological strides made but also the challenges faced in the realm of auditory computing. Critical questions revolve around achieving high accuracy in sound recognition across diverse conditions, ensuring user privacy in audio data processing, and developing auditory interfaces that are adaptable and inclusive. Additionally, the integration of auditory computing into assistive technology has profound implications for accessibility, providing tools for individuals with disabilities to engage more fully with digital ecosystems. A comprehensive review of current literature and technological trends shows an urgent need for innovative approaches that anticipate and overcome these challenges. Through interdisciplinary collaboration between computer scientists, sound engineers, and human-computer interaction experts, the potential for developing sophisticated auditory interfaces is vast. This paper aims to unravel the complexities and promise of these technologies, advocating for more widespread and effective adoption in the coming years.
