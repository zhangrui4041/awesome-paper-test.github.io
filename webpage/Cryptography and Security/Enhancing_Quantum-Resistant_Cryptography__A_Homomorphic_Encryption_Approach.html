Introduction

In recent years, the rapid advancement of quantum computing technology has brought new challenges to the field of cryptography and security of computer systems. Quantum computers, with their immense computational power, are expected to solve complex mathematical problems much faster than classical computers, threatening the integrity of current cryptographic systems. Traditional encryption algorithms such as RSA and ECC rely on the difficulty of specific mathematical problems that quantum computers are believed to solve efficiently using Shor's algorithm. This potential breach of security protocols has sparked a global interest in developing quantum-resistant cryptographic methods. One promising avenue in this emerging research landscape is homomorphic encryption, which enables computations on encrypted data without needing decryption. This characteristic preserves data confidentiality while allowing computation on sensitive information. Combining homomorphic encryption with lattice-based cryptography—known for its resistance to quantum attacks—presents an attractive solution to the imminent threats posed by quantum technologies. This paper aims to enhance quantum-resistant cryptography by developing a framework that integrates homomorphic encryption into existing lattice-based cryptographic systems. Our research explores the theoretical and practical implications of such integration, including efficiency improvements and security enhancements compared to traditional cryptographic approaches. We begin by reviewing the current state of quantum-resistant cryptography, highlighting various theoretical concepts and practical implementations of homomorphic encryption and lattice-based approaches. This literature review forms the basis for understanding the motivation behind our research and underlines the urgency of the threats to classical cryptographic systems. We then present our proposed framework and demonstrate its viability through experimental results, showing promise for robust and secure computation in a post-quantum world. Our research not only contributes to the growing field of post-quantum cryptography but also anticipates future security needs, ensuring that our digital communications and stored data remain secure in the face of quantum advancements. Through this work, we hope to pave the way for further innovations that can effectively counteract the looming threat of quantum-enabled cryptographic breaches.
