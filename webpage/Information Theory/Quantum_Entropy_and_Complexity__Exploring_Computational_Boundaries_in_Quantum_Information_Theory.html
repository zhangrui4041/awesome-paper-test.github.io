Introduction

The advancement of quantum computing presents both opportunities and challenges in the landscape of computational sciences. As the field ventures beyond classical boundaries, the conceptual understanding of information theory necessitates re-examination. This paper delves into two pivotal constructs: entropy and complexity, notions that are fundamentally intertwined with the computational prowess and limitations of quantum systems. Entropy, as a measure of uncertainty and information content, is a cornerstone concept in information theory. Within quantum mechanics, entropy takes on a new dimension as reflected in Von Neumann entropy, which assesses the information encoded in quantum states. By examining how entropy functions in quantum contexts, we gain insights into the potential of quantum entropy measures to redefine our understanding of information limits and channel capacity in quantum processes. Complexity theory, which studies computational efficiency and feasibility, is equally vital in understanding quantum information processing. Traditional complexity measures need adaptation to party with quantum behaviors, leading to the concept of quantum Kolmogorov complexity. This measure captures the minimal descriptions needed to encode quantum states, elucidating the intricacies and resource demands of quantum algorithms. This paper synthesizes these areas—quantum entropy and quantum complexity—building bridges between theoretical constructs and practical applications. In doing so, we highlight the emerging quantum advantage in information processing, setting the stage for innovations in solving classically intractable problems. Our research aims not merely to chart these computing boundaries, but also to propose methods for exploiting quantum properties. As the quantum computational era unfolds, addressing these theoretical challenges is crucial. This exploration, therefore, paves the way for future breakthroughs in quantum computing, paving paths for significant theoretical and technological advancements within computer science.
