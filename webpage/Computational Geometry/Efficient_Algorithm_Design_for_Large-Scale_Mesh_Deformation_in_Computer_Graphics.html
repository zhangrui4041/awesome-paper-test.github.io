Introduction

In the rapidly evolving field of computer graphics, the manipulation of large-scale geometric datasets presents a formidable challenge. The demand for real-time interactive simulations and high-fidelity visualizations has surged, particularly with the rise of virtual reality and complex digital environments. Central to this domain is the task of mesh deformation, which involves altering the vertices of 3D models to achieve desired shapes or animations without compromising on quality or computational efficiency. Traditionally, mesh handling has been plagued by considerable computational cost, primarily due to the sheer volume of vertices and the intricacies involved in maintaining mesh integrity throughout transformations. Existing approaches, often rooted in linear algebra and numerical optimization, have provided solutions, but they frequently fall short when scaling up to large, intricate models. These methods either sacrifice real-time capability or fail to preserve important geometric details, obstacles that this paper seeks to overcome. This paper proposes a new framework for mesh deformation grounded in computational geometry principles that aim to transcend the limitations of previous methods. We draw inspiration from graph-based structures and adaptive spatial partitions to create a mechanism capable of handling complex deformations efficiently. By using dynamic data structures like KD-trees and bounding volume hierarchies, our algorithm dynamically adjusts to the mesh's structure, which reduces unnecessary computations and enhances responsiveness. This approach not only accelerates the processing time but also ensures that vital geometric aspects, such as sharp edges and textures, remain intact. Our contribution is substantiated by comprehensive testing on diverse datasets that simulate both regular and irregular geometries encountered in contemporary graphics applications. These tests highlight the algorithm's robustness, efficiency, and scalability, making it a promising tool for developers and researchers aiming to push the boundaries of computational graphics. With potential applications ranging from interactive gaming environments to sophisticated archviz and CAD systems, our findings are poised to facilitate significant advancements in the creation and manipulation of digital spaces. This work endeavors to lay the groundwork for future innovations in mesh processing, heralding a new era of efficiency and realism in computational geometry.
